Privacy Policy and Terms of Use Update

It has been 9 months since we last updated our terms and privacy policy, and as we continue to build out CM and the services we provide it’s time for another round of updates. As we don’t collect email addresses for our users, we can’t do the usual ‘spam the update to everyone’ that most companies do, so this blog post will serve that purpose. So let’s get this started.

OUR TERMS HAVE CHANGED EFFECTIVE IMMEDIATELY FOR USERS WHO USE THIS SITE (AND OUR SERVICES AND PRODUCTS) ON OR AFTER THE DATE ABOVE AND ON JULY 4th , 2014, FOR PREVIOUS USERS OF THIS SITE AND OUR SERVICES AND PRODUCTS).

As our users, these updates are important for you to understand, so we’ll take a brief look at what they mean.

Privacy Policy – We’ve updated our Privacy Policy to provide additional information regarding what personal information we collect and how we use it. This covers the anonymous statistics collection seen in CM as well as additional information you provide through services such as CM Account or a larger scale Cyanogen Store.

Third Parties – As Cyanogen Inc. continues to expand, they enter agreements with the likes of Google and other parties for services seen on retail hardware. Language has been added to reflect both the Terms and Privacy Policy as it relates to third parties.

User Generated Content – Most relevant for forums, communities and other areas where you may provide content to share, we want to make sure this content is not offensive to other users. The terms now carries a section to reflect this content and our use of it.

Arbitration  – We’ve added an arbitration clause to our Terms. While we don’t anticipate legal action, this provides a quicker and more efficient way to resolve any potential legal disputes, and skips the long process of courts.

You can read the full Terms of Use and Privacy Policy at the links provided. We’ve done our best to steer clear of too much legal verbiage and clarify as much as possible. We will continue to secure your information and we don’t sell it to third parties for marketing.

  • CrookedKnight

    If Cyanogen does anything worth suing over — say, they start selling your private information to third parties even though the Privacy Policy says they can’t – you are no longer allowed to sue them in an actual court, unless you’re going after damages so small that they belong in small-claims court.

    Instead, you get to go to binding arbitration, where there’s no option for jury trial, no appeal, no oversight by any public official…basically, no guarantee that the law will be followed. But it’s faster and cheaper than going before an actual judge, so it saves money for Cyanogen and its investors.

    Oh, and you also have to give up your right to participate in a class-action case. If something bad were to happen on a large scale – for instance, if a supposedly “stable” CM release permanently bricked every device it was installed to – every single person affected would have to sue separately.

    If you live in a country where your right to court access and/or class-action litigation is protected by law, then this doesn’t affect you. But the United States is not such a country. You can thank the Supreme Court for that!
